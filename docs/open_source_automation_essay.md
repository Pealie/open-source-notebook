# Open source as a public strategy in the age of automation

The most consequential technology story of the coming decade is not a new gadget. It is the quiet, compounding spread of automation into the routines that keep firms and public services running: billing, scheduling, procurement, logistics, compliance, basic analysis, customer contact, and parts of design and engineering. What began as robotics in factories and scripts in back offices is now swelling into systems that can observe, decide, and act across entire workflows. The promise is higher productivity, fewer errors, faster throughput, and machines that predict failure before it happens. The risk is that the gains arrive unevenly and the costs are socialised.

Forecasts vary, but the direction is consistent. The World Economic Forum expects substantial churn by 2030: it anticipates the displacement of the equivalent of around 8% of current jobs (about 92 million), alongside job creation that yields net growth of around 7% (about 78 million). The IMF has argued that AI will affect almost 40% of jobs globally, and up to 60% of jobs in advanced economies, with the balance between augmentation and replacement shaping inequality and social stability. These are scenario-based estimates rather than prophecies, but they are sober enough to justify a practical question for policymakers and employers alike: what kind of technology ecosystem best cushions disruption while still capturing productivity gains?

One answer, not fashionable enough in boardrooms, is governance of the underlying tools. In that conversation, open source is not a hobbyist creed. It is an economic instrument.

## What open source actually changes

Open source is often reduced to 'free software'. Cost matters, but the deeper change is permission: permission to inspect, modify, share, and build upon code and designs under an explicit licence. That permission moves control from a single vendor to an ecosystem, which alters incentives in ways that become more important as automation becomes pervasive.

**Transparency and accountability.** In an automated workplace, software is no longer merely a neutral tool. It becomes a decision-maker. When systems determine who gets paid, what gets ordered, which work is prioritised, or whether a machine is 'healthy' enough to keep running, accountability becomes a civic issue. Open code cannot guarantee ethical outcomes, but it enables audit. It allows independent scrutiny of what a system does and why, and it shortens the distance between a problem being discovered and a fix being made. In a world of opaque vendor logic, the only remedy can be a contract negotiation. In a world where code can be read, the remedy can be engineering.

**Competition and bargaining power.** Proprietary platforms can lock organisations into pricing, upgrade cycles, data formats, and roadmaps that primarily serve shareholders. Open source tends to favour exit, because switching is easier when interfaces are documented and implementations exist. Over time, this disciplines suppliers and encourages a market of services, integration, hosting, and support rather than a market of captive customers. It also reduces the risk that a single platform becomes the toll booth through which an entire sector must pass.

**Capability and skills.** When code is open, learning is not limited to a vendor's training course. Apprenticeship becomes literal: systems can be studied, forked, instrumented, and improved. That matters in an era when retraining is not optional. Open ecosystems can turn disruption into a ladder, because skills acquired in one setting transfer elsewhere. Skills that are portable are also bargaining tools, which is a subtle but meaningful counterweight to labour market volatility.

**Resilience and strategic autonomy.** Software is infrastructure. When it fails, societies feel it. Open source often becomes the shared substrate across firms and borders because it is inspectable, portable, and widely maintained. A strong open ecosystem reduces single points of failure, including geopolitical ones, by lowering dependence on any one supplier or jurisdiction. It also reduces fragility created by closed formats and undocumented behaviours, which can turn routine upgrades into existential risks.

There is an economic argument here, not merely a moral one. The European Commission's study on open source software and hardware estimated that open source contributes between €65 billion and €95 billion annually to EU GDP, and suggested that increasing contributions could yield sizeable returns through spillovers into innovation and competitiveness. In other words, openness can be treated as a lever for productivity and competitiveness, not only as an ideology.

## Automation is a stack, not a product

Automation is frequently discussed as a product category: robotics, RPA, industrial control platforms, 'agentic' systems. The deeper reality is a stack that runs from chips to operating systems to libraries to data infrastructure to applications. Open source already dominates crucial layers of that stack, which means the debate is not about whether open source exists. It is about where in the stack openness is allowed to stop.

In business automation, closed systems concentrate power at the level of workflow logic and data. A firm can become a tenant on someone else's platform for the basic operations of an industry, with switching costs that are operational rather than merely financial. When those platforms incorporate AI, the logic can become harder to contest, because decisions are presented as probabilistic and emergent rather than rule-based and explicit. Open implementations of workflow engines, analytics pipelines, and integration middleware shift that balance. They create room for smaller suppliers, local integrators, and in-house teams. They also make it easier to build 'compliance by design' into systems that span procurement, risk, and reporting, because the compliance logic can be reviewed and tested rather than trusted.

In industrial automation, openness has an additional benefit: interoperability. Factories, utilities, and transport systems are long-lived and heterogeneous. Plants run equipment across decades, with layers of retrofits and custom interfaces. Open reference implementations can anchor common protocols and data models, making it possible to connect old assets to new monitoring and optimisation systems without paying a perpetual translation tax to proprietary gateways. This is not romance. It is maintenance economics. It is the difference between automation being an expensive bespoke retrofit and automation being a composable upgrade.

Openness also matters for safety. When automation governs physical systems, the stakes are higher than convenience. Auditability, reproducibility, and independent verification become normal requirements. Open source is not a substitute for standards and certification, but it can make them easier to meet, because the artefacts being assessed are visible and testable. Safety cases improve when a system's behaviour can be reproduced across environments, and when independent auditors can inspect the code and build chain rather than relying on assurances.

## The social bargain: who captures the productivity dividend?

The core political tension of automation is distribution. Productivity rises, but wages and opportunity do not automatically follow. A world of closed automation stacks can exacerbate this by funnelling rents to a small number of platform owners, even while workers and small firms absorb the adjustment costs. The same pattern has played out repeatedly in the platform economy: network effects create concentration, and concentration turns technical control into pricing power.

Open source tends to diffuse value. It does not eliminate profit, but it changes where profit arises. Money moves from licences to services, from monopoly pricing to differentiated expertise. That supports local and sectoral autonomy: a hospital trust, council, manufacturer, or SME can share improvements, pool maintenance, and avoid duplicating the same expensive integration work. It also makes it easier for public institutions and smaller firms to participate in modernisation without handing over core operational data and process logic to a single vendor.

This diffusion is precisely what a disruptive period demands. If automation is going to rewrite job roles, then broad access to the tools of automation becomes a matter of economic resilience. Without it, the transition risks becoming a one-way extraction of labour into capital: fewer roles, higher profits, and weak pathways back into decent work for those displaced.

Honesty is still required. Open source can also accelerate adoption because it lowers barriers. That can increase displacement in the short run. The point is not to slow change by restricting tools, which rarely works for long. The point is to keep the new machinery of work contestable, so that policy, labour, and civil society can shape outcomes rather than merely react to them after the fact.

## The hard parts: sustainability, security, and governance

Open source is not automatically benevolent. It can be underfunded, poorly governed, and insecure. The free rider problem is real: many organisations depend on projects maintained by a handful of people, while the benefits accrue to thousands. The modern software supply chain has also shown how attackers can target widely used components by attempting to compromise them upstream. Reporting on sabotage attempts, including the XZ Utils backdoor attempt, illustrated how strategic and patient such attacks can be.

This is why the argument for broader adoption must be paired with an argument for investment and discipline. Policy moves on software supply chain security point in that direction. The US Executive Order on improving the nation's cybersecurity and related guidance from NIST have treated software provenance and secure development as resilience issues, encouraging practices such as component inventories and secure development baselines. The NIST Secure Software Development Framework codifies baseline secure development practices that can be applied across both proprietary and open ecosystems.

Open source is well suited to this world because it can support reproducible builds, independent audits, and shared security tooling. It is also the focus of security-specific collaborations that aim to harden widely used projects. But suitability is not enough. The ecosystem has to be treated as infrastructure. Critical components cannot depend on goodwill alone.

Sustainability is therefore not a minor afterthought. It is the central test of seriousness. If open source is part of the backbone of modern automation, then maintainers should not be expected to hold that weight alone. Funding, governance, and security operations have to be built into the story.

## What wider adoption should look like

A more general adoption of open source is not a demand that everything be open. It is a proposal for default settings and public interest guardrails.

**1) Public procurement should be open by preference.** When public money funds software, the public should not repeatedly pay for the same capabilities behind different proprietary walls. Procurement can prioritise open standards, data portability, and open licensing where appropriate, while still allowing proprietary components when there is a clear case. The aim is competition on quality and service, not lock-in by design.

**2) Fund maintenance, not just innovation.** Grants and industrial policy often reward new projects and neglect the boring work that keeps systems safe. Funding bodies can support core maintainers, security audits, documentation, testing infrastructure, and release engineering. Targeted investment in security hardening and automated analysis is a rational form of risk management, particularly for widely deployed libraries and tooling.

**3) Require supply chain transparency for critical systems.** For automation systems that affect safety, public welfare, or core economic infrastructure, component transparency and secure development practices should be baseline expectations regardless of licensing. Open source makes compliance easier, but it should not be optional.

**4) Build open training pathways around real code.** Reskilling programmes are more credible when they teach with the tools that industry actually uses. Open source allows curricula to track reality. It also lets workers build portfolios that travel across employers, which matters in a volatile labour market. In practical terms, open ecosystems create more entry points into technical work: documentation, testing, support, localisation, integration, and operations are employable skills.

**5) Treat interoperability as a competition policy tool.** In automation, the ability to move data and replace components determines who has leverage. Regulators can encourage open interfaces and challenge anti-competitive lock-in, especially where a platform becomes the de facto operating system for an industry.

**6) Use openness to strengthen scrutiny in AI.** Where AI systems are used to allocate resources, determine access, or shape employment decisions, independent evaluation matters. Full openness is not universally appropriate, but transparency about training data provenance, model behaviour, evaluation results, and decision logic should be considered part of the social licence to deploy.

## A pragmatic conclusion

Automation is coming regardless of licensing. The open question is whether it will arrive as a closed empire or a shared commons. Open source will not prevent job losses or make every deployment ethical. What it can do is widen participation in the productivity dividend, reduce dependency on a small set of vendors, and make the most powerful systems easier to scrutinise.

In a period of rapid industrial and business transformation, open source is less a philosophy than a stabiliser. It is a way to keep the machinery of innovation from becoming a private toll road, and to ensure that societies retain the capability to understand and reshape the tools that increasingly shape work.
