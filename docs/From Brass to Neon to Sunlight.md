**From Brass to Neon to Sunlight: Steampunk, Cyberpunk, Solarpunk and the Future After Hope**

Steampunk, cyberpunk and solarpunk are often treated as adjacent labels within speculative fiction, but they can also be understood as a sequence of cultural moods, each registering a different historical experience of technology. They are not merely styles of costume, architecture or visual design, though each possesses a recognisable aesthetic surface. More deeply, they are literary modes through which successive generations have imagined the relation between power, machinery, labour, the body and the future. Read in sequence, they form a revealing arc: from the re-enchanted machine of industrial modernity, through the disillusioned circuitry of late capitalism, to the ecologically reparative horizon of post-carbon imagination.

Steampunk begins as a backward-looking futurism. It turns to the nineteenth century, or a version of it, and discovers there a field of speculative possibility. Its worlds are filled with engines, locomotives, airships, automata, goggles, brass instruments and laboratory apparatus, but these are not only decorative features. They embody a fantasy of technological legibility. The machine in steampunk is visible, tactile and, at least in principle, understandable. One can open it, repair it, listen to it, feel its heat. That material intimacy is crucial to the genre’s appeal. In an age increasingly dominated by opaque digital systems, steampunk offered a retrospective dream of technology as something with surfaces, weight and audible force.

Yet steampunk’s backward glance is never wholly innocent. The century it reimagines was also the age of empire, colonial expansion, industrial exploitation and rigid social hierarchy. The polished brass and hand-crafted leather of steampunk can all too easily slide into nostalgia for a world whose elegance was inseparable from extraction and violence. For that reason, the most substantial works in the genre do not simply luxuriate in Victoriana. They ask what industrial modernity cost, who paid for it and how its machinery of progress was entangled with domination. Steampunk at its strongest is therefore double in vision: enchanted by material invention, suspicious of the historical order that framed it. It is less an exercise in costume than a way of revisiting the origins of modern technological consciousness.

Cyberpunk arrives as the dark inversion of that sensibility. If steampunk loves visible engines, cyberpunk is haunted by invisible systems. The machine no longer sits outside the human world as an object of fascination. It has entered the bloodstream of daily life. It is embedded in code, networks, implants, surveillance architectures, financial systems and corporate infrastructures. The cityscape of cyberpunk is not the workshop or salon, but the dense, illuminated, privatised metropolis, where data and capital move faster than law and where bodies are porous to technical intervention.

The shift from steampunk to cyberpunk is not merely a change in décor from brass to chrome. It marks a deeper transformation in how power is imagined. In steampunk, power tends to be embodied in empires, factories, engines, inventors and institutions. In cyberpunk, power becomes diffuse, informational and ambient. It is distributed across corporations, algorithms, communication networks and systems of control too vast to confront directly. Hence the characteristic cyberpunk protagonist: not the explorer or engineer in the heroic mode, but the hacker, courier, mercenary or drifter surviving at the edge of structures that cannot be meaningfully reformed. Cyberpunk’s world is one in which technical sophistication has not brought liberation, but intensified inequality, commodification and alienation.

Its famous formula, ‘high tech, low life’, remains one of the sharpest summaries of late twentieth-century technological modernity. Cyberpunk does not reject technology. On the contrary, it is intoxicated by it. But it strips away the old optimism that equated innovation with moral progress. Bodies can be enhanced and still remain disposable. Consciousness can be networked and still remain lonely. Information can become nearly infinite while justice becomes more remote. In literary terms, cyberpunk develops a prose of compression, overload and sensory saturation, matching the intensity of the worlds it describes. Its streets blaze with signs, screens and weather; its characters move through a permanent present of transaction, improvisation and threat. It is the noir of digital capitalism.

Solarpunk emerges in response to the exhaustion of that noir. It is not a denial of crisis, but a refusal of despair as the only credible tone for the future. If cyberpunk became the dominant style for imagining technologically saturated life under neoliberalism, solarpunk attempts to recover the possibility of futurity from the grip of collapse. Born in the shadow of climate emergency, ecological degradation and infrastructural breakdown, it insists that speculative literature must do more than diagnose catastrophe. It must also imagine forms of repair.

This does not make solarpunk merely cheerful. Its brightness is hard won. The genre is animated by the knowledge that existing systems are damaging, unequal and unsustainable. But where cyberpunk treats those systems as nearly total, solarpunk seeks spaces in which they can be redesigned, resisted or replaced. Its central images are accordingly different: distributed energy, walkable cities, adaptive buildings, rooftop gardens, reclaimed materials, co-operatives, shared workshops, community agriculture, open technical knowledge. The movement from cyberpunk to solarpunk is therefore not simply an aesthetic one. It is a shift in narrative and political emphasis, from infiltration to cultivation, from survival within hostile systems to the patient construction of more livable ones.

That shift poses a formal challenge. Literature has long found catastrophe easier to dramatise than care. Violence, conspiracy, chase and collapse generate immediate narrative tension, whereas maintenance, reciprocity and stewardship are slower arts. Solarpunk’s significance lies partly in its willingness to accept that difficulty. It asks whether beauty, repair and interdependence can become serious literary subjects without dissolving into naivety. At its best, it answers yes. It inherits from steampunk a delight in designed environments and from cyberpunk a scepticism toward concentrated power, but it refuses both imperial romance and terminal cynicism. It argues that hope can be intellectually rigorous, that the imagination of viable worlds need not be sentimental merely because it is not apocalyptic.

Seen as a sequence, these three genres also register changing forms of materiality. Steampunk dwells among metals, pistons, leather, steam, wood, gauges and glass. Cyberpunk dissolves much of that solidity into information, screens, code and synthetic surfaces, even as its cities remain grimly physical at the level of deprivation. Solarpunk rematerialises the world again, but in another key: sunlight, algae, timber, reclaimed concrete, passive cooling, water capture, repairable fabrication, plant life integrated with infrastructure. Its beauty lies in coexistence rather than ornament, in the attempt to imagine built environments that participate in ecological process instead of mastering it by force.

What, then, might come after solarpunk?

The answer can only be provisional, since genres acquire names only after their pressures have begun to crystallise. Yet it seems increasingly likely that the next speculative mood will arise from a crisis not only of energy or ecology, but of perception itself. If steampunk responded to industrial modernity, cyberpunk to digital capitalism, and solarpunk to climate crisis, the emerging genre may respond to the destabilisation of reality under conditions of artificial cognition, synthetic media and algorithmic mediation.

One might tentatively call this successor ‘cognitionpunk’, though the label matters less than the problem it names. Such a genre would concern itself with mental sovereignty, epistemic trust, ambient simulation and the fate of human interiority in worlds where intelligent systems do not merely surround life but interpret it continuously. Its anxiety would not be limited to surveillance in the familiar cyberpunk sense, nor to ecological breakdown in the solarpunk sense, but would focus instead on the erosion of shared reality. What becomes of memory, witness, authorship, authenticity and moral judgement when every image can be fabricated, every voice mimicked, every environment personalised and every preference nudged by unseen systems of optimisation?

This possible next genre would differ from cyberpunk in an important way. Cyberpunk often dramatise the digital as a heightened realm, a special zone of entry, risk and transgression. A post-solarpunk literature of cognition would likely treat mediation as ordinary, seamless and nearly total. Its settings might be less flamboyantly dystopian and therefore more disquieting. The conflict would turn on the defence of unmanipulated attention, the preservation of ambiguity against prediction, and the struggle to maintain common worlds in the face of algorithmically tailored experience. Its protagonists might not be hackers in the classical sense, but custodians of reality: archivists, witnesses, teachers, small publishers, local communities, ritual keepers, designers of spaces resistant to optimisation. Resistance, in such writing, might take the form of slowness, opacity, embodied presence, analogue residue or collective acts of verification.

At the same time, this speculative successor would probably inherit solarpunk’s refusal of pure despair. It would not simply lament artificiality or dream of retreat into some untouched human essence. Rather, it would ask how coexistence with intelligent systems might be rendered answerable to truth, reciprocity and consciousness. In that sense, the next genre after solarpunk may be less about rejecting technology than about contesting the colonisation of perception by systems designed to predict, persuade and pre-empt.

The movement from steampunk to cyberpunk to solarpunk is therefore not best understood as a neat progression of subgenres, but as a running argument about what technology means to culture at different historical moments. Steampunk reopens the age of engines in order to ask how modernity first enchanted itself. Cyberpunk records the capture of the future by code, capital and urban abstraction. Solarpunk insists that imagination must once again become capable of repair. The genre that follows may have to undertake an even more delicate task: defending human depth and shared reality in a world where consciousness itself has become a design surface.

What unites all these modes is their concern with permission. Steampunk grants permission to romanticise the machine, though often under the shadow of critique. Cyberpunk grants permission to distrust the machine and the systems it serves. Solarpunk grants permission to imagine that another relation to technology might still be possible. The next genre, whatever name it eventually acquires, may need to grant permission for something more difficult still: to believe that truth, attention and interior freedom remain worth fighting for in an age of total mediation.

In that sense, the future after solarpunk may not be a brighter utopia or a darker dystopia, but a literature of contested reality. Its central question will not simply be how we power the world, but how we perceive it, share it and remain answerable to one another within it. That would be a fitting successor to the line that runs from steam to code to sunlight, for it would recognise that the deepest technological struggle ahead may not concern machinery alone, but the conditions under which experience itself remains human.
